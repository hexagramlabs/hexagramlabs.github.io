Internet Service Providers

2025/08/07

Today I learned that some of the laws claiming to protect “free markets” in the internet provider industry are actually the reason I’m paying twice as much for worse service—and why I can’t switch.

In some locations, laws give a single Internet Service Provider an exclusive territory, blocking competitors entirely. In other instances, a web of incentives are the reason a single companies dominate an area. As a counter-example, at my previous home’s address, various internet providers were allowed to service the area. Therefore, potential customers get to engage with a free market and pick the service that works best for them. At that particular location, it’s possible to get fiber optic internet service (500 Mbps) at just $45 per month. This outcome is only possible due to various companies being able to compete with one another.

But in the new area I moved to—only a mere three miles away from my previous address—there’s basically only one company that specializes in providing internet service. That company charges me about $100 a month for a DSL line (249 Mbps) that doesn’t come close to the fiber plan available just a few blocks over at my old address.

So, why do local governments in some areas grant certain companies exclusive rights to service particular zones? This seems to be a thing that’s often billed as a way to avoid having to build out additional infrastructure, or preserve free markets, or save money. Or something. But the effect is precisely the opposite.

Additionally, there are municipal broadband limits at the state level, so-called public “broadband restrictions.” These frameworks drastically limit municipalities from public broadband expansion in the name of preserving both costs and preventing “unfair competition.”

Unsurprisingly, legislation of this nature is often the result of lobbying. Incumbent internet service providers want to avoid competing with other companies that might offer faster or cheaper alternatives.

For example, Charter Communications, also known as Spectrum, ranks at around 34 out of 9400 companies tracked by OpenSecrets, having spent nearly $11 million dollars in lobbying—and helping keep competitors out. Their company’s revenue in 2024 was approximately $55.09 billion.

The competition with Spectrum, at least where I live, is largely between the local city’s offering and Frontier Communications, a much smaller publicly traded utility company. Frontier Communications revenue in 2021 was around $6.41 billion.

Current laws around this stuff are often so vague in many places they essentially outright forbid broadband expansion, such that would-be competitors are barred from ever having the opportunity to compete with incumbent ones. As a consequence, Spectrum remains the only available internet service provider in many areas, including mine.

The situation kind of resembles a monopoly. Maybe it more closely resembles an exclusive franchise or “de facto monopoly.” But when you consider the lobbying relations between private companies, such as Spectrum, and the government, I suppose it can begin to look somewhat more like a monopoly.

But regardless, with the broadband restrictions in place, ISPs like Spectrum use their lobbying power to preserve the current policy, thereby avoiding competition in certain geographic areas. It’s like gerrymandering, but for internet access.

The main point is, this mess keeps me locked into paying $100 a month for a service that is both drastically worse and more expensive than competitors who are allowed to operate just a few miles away.

Some states have begun to repeal broadband restrictions, enabling more competitive markets, and sometimes higher-quality internet service for lower prices. But there are still quite a few states where internet access continues to be in a state of regulatory capture.

But it makes me wonder–how many other regulatory frameworks hide behind “free market” rhetoric to block competition, inflate prices, and limit choice?
